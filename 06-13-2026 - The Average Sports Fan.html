The “Average” Sports Fan

June 13, 2026

Envision the typical United States sports fan.

Across all sports leagues in the United States, the image remains oddly specific: 40 / 50 years old. Mainly male, white. Sometimes with their son or daughter. Maybe wearing a jersey that is 15 years old. Maybe sitting in the same spot they have watched games from for the last decade. Maybe yelling at the TV as if the coach can hear them and would listen.

This is the person the leagues USED to sell to us.

The loyal fan. The diehard. The local guy. The person who watches every regular season game even when the team is 23-41. The person who knows the backup point guard, the third line center, the middle reliever, the cornerback who only plays special teams. The person 30 years from now who relates his childhood to sports. The person who feels insane for caring this much, but continues to care anyway.

Yet when the games start to matter most, heck even a LITTLE BIT, that person disappears.

Why are the NBA Finals priced at $10k a ticket?

Why are the best seats filled with celebrities?

Why are the broadcasts more interested in showing Taylor Swift, Ben Stiller, Drake, Jack Nicholson, or whoever else happens to be courtside than the actual fans who lived and died with the team all season? Better yet, why are they even there? For free too, according to Stiller himself.

Why is the average working man excluded?

This is not even really about the celebrities themselves. I understand why they are there. They have money. They have status. They make the event look bigger. It’s exposure for them. A Finals game becomes less of a basketball game and more of a red carpet with a scoreboard attached.

And that is exactly what my issue is.

Sports are supposed to be the last remaining place where status does not matter. You do not need to be rich to know ball. You do not need a title to understand a blown coverage. You do not need a certain net worth to feel your stomach drop when your team misses a free throw, or for the excitement for when a huge trade is being worked on.

The game itself is supposed to be meritocracy.

The better player wins. The tougher team wins. The smarter coach wins. The fans scream. The arena shakes. Everyone becomes part of something bigger than themselves.

In reality, the closer a team gets to a championship, the less the event belongs to the real fan. The regular season (at least the first few months) is for loyalists. The rest is for the corporate class.

Athletes, and I believe I am qualified to call myself that to an extent, always prefer the passion. Always.

There is nothing like playing in front of people who actually care. People who are angry when you lose. People who feel insulted when the team does not compete. People who are willing to do anything to win, even though they are not physically on the roster. Your parents, friends, neighborhood, children that want to grow up and be you.

That sounds irrational because it is irrational.

But that is sports.

The real fan does not just feel like they are watching the team. They believe they are part of the team. They say “we” because the team has occupied a piece of their identity for years. They say “we need to rebound better.” “We cannot turn the ball over.” “We need one stop.”

The fan is not on the payroll. The fan is not in the huddle. The fan is not taking the shot.

Yet emotionally, it is completely real.

That is what makes sports different from normal entertainment. A person does not usually say “we” after watching a movie. Nobody walks out of a concert saying “we really performed well tonight.” But sports create this strange ownership. A fake ownership, maybe, but a meaningful one.

And leagues exploit this.

They use the loyalty. They sell the jerseys. They sell the commercials about fathers and sons. They show the old fan crying after 40 years of heartbreak. They market the idea that this team belongs to the city, the family, the neighborhood, the ordinary person.

Then, once the team is actually good, they raise the bridge.

The ordinary person can watch from home.

The celebrity can sit courtside.

The banker can expense it.

The influencer can post it.

The longtime fan can maybe get a standing-room-only ticket if they want to destroy their rent payment.

This is where the whole thing starts to feel fake.

Even the game feels fake. Rigged. A show.

The incentives around it feel fake.

The broadcast wants celebrities because celebrities create secondary content. The league wants high ticket prices because scarcity creates prestige and value. The arena wants corporate buyers because corporate buyers do not flinch. They have an endless supply of money to pay. The sponsors want a luxury product because luxury products attract luxury margins. More profits.

That all makes business sense, but business sense and fan sense are not the same thing.

The Super Bowl is barely a football game anymore. It is a national corporate holiday. The World Series has the same problem. European soccer has been fighting this for years, especially with ownership groups that treat historic clubs like private equity assets (see the Premier League). Even college sports, which used to feel slightly more tied to community, is now openly professional, openly transactional, and openly chasing television money. NIL is all you hear about, and rightly so given its impact.

I am not naïve and I know that money has always existed in sports. Rich people have always had good seats. Owners have always been owners. The average fan was never truly in control.

But something feels different now.

Maybe it is the ticket prices becoming so absurd that attendance itself becomes a status signal.

Maybe it is the fact that the leagues still advertise working-class loyalty while constructing an in-person product that working-class people cannot access.

Sports need the average fan spiritually, but not financially. Emotion, but not his presence. Loyalty, but not his seat.

And maybe that is why the celebrity reaction shots are so annoying. It is not because people hate celebrities although I am not exactly a huge fan myself.

It is because every time the camera cuts to one, it reminds everyone watching at home that the best seat in the building did not go to the person who cared the most.

It went to the person who made the event look the most expensive.

The irony is that leagues are obsessed with “growing the game,” but sometimes the best way to grow the game is to stop treating the real fan like a background character. Stop pretending the soul of the sport is in the luxury suite.

The average fan may not make the broadcast sexier, be Instagram famous, or have a brand partnership.

But he is the one who remembers the bad years, cared when nobody else did, and passes the team down to his kids.

He is the one who actually believes.

Losing him doesn’t lose meaningful revenue, sure, but it loses the soul of sports.
