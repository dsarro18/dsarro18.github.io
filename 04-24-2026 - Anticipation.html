Anticipation

April 24, 2026

Stimulation has broken a core tenant of humanity: anticipation.

The oft-discussed effects of cell phones and social media revolve around attention spans, but that’s a surface-level diagnosis. The real loss is deeper. We’ve lost the ability to feel excitement for something that hasn’t happened yet.

We didn’t just lose patience — we lost the future as a feeling.

This ultimately breaks into two parts:
1) Excitement is real
2) Disappointment is necessary

Both are required for human emotion. Both regulate each other. And both are, in their own way, good.

Anticipation creates excitement. Expectation creates disappointment. You need both.

I watched a flashback on YouTube centered around “Remember the Hype” for Star Wars: Episode I – The Phantom Menace and The Dark Knight (yes, my YouTube recommended is unique).

Merchandising, TV spots, message boards, early internet buzz—this is 1997 and 2008, long before today’s version of social media. It felt like a time capsule.

I stepped back and pretended I didn’t know what would happen. That alone felt foreign.

It was impossible to fully predict how either movie would land. The gap between expectation and reality was wide enough for anticipation to exist.

And the outcomes couldn’t have been more different.

Phantom Menace “flopped” culturally while dominating the box office. It became the weakest of the original six, damaged George Lucas’ legacy, and still lives on through memes decades later. It killed anticipation for what came next.

The Dark Knight did the opposite. A masterpiece. It cemented Christopher Nolan’s legacy and redefined the ceiling for crime drama. It still generates new interpretations and discussion nearly two decades later.

But the key point isn’t the outcome, but rather that both had real anticipation.

That feeling is gone today.

Anticipation requires constraint, and stimulation removes constraint.

In the past, information was limited. Access was delayed. You had to wait. That waiting period was filled with imagination. You projected what something could be.

Now, everything is previewed, leaked, analyzed, and clipped before it even arrives. You don’t anticipate the experience anymore.

The gap between “before” and “after” has collapsed. And without that gap, anticipation cannot exist.

This doesn’t just apply to movies. It applies to everything.

Relationships: You don’t wonder anymore. You text. You check. You know.

Travel: You’ve already seen every street on TikTok before you arrive.

Food: You don’t build toward a meal — you scroll options and have it instantly.

Careers: You jump to the next opportunity the moment friction appears.

Anticipation used to be embedded in the structure of life.

There’s a second-order effect here that’s more subtle.

When anticipation disappears, so does emotional amplitude.

Excitement flattens because nothing builds. Disappointment flattens because nothing was truly expected.

You end up in a constant middle state: stimulated, but never fully engaged.

There is always an alternative. Usually worse. Always available. And people flock to it anyway.

Anticipation was never just about waiting.

It was about believing something ahead of you might matter.

And if everything can be experienced instantly, nothing is worth waiting for.

And if nothing is worth waiting for, nothing feels earned.

And if nothing feels earned, nothing feels significant.

And as a result, we lost our ability to feel something.
