The World You Grew Up In No Longer Exists

March 7, 2026

“The world you grew up in no longer exists”
“The world you remember no longer exists”
“The world you idolized no longer exists”

These quote variations all express the same idea: replacement. Replacement of ideas, values, demographics, goals, viewpoints…

The implications convey a sense of hope, despair, nervousness, and curiosity while the feeling is dependent on the user and context within that generation.

It is impossible to pinpoint one singular event as the “turning point” of humanity nor will I attempt to define or rank relevant events.

However, I increasingly find myself grappling with one specific development that has shaped the world that I grew up with and will die with: globalization.

Transport back to the 1990’s. Globalization was sold as the exchange of ideas, both cultural and economic, to enrich the betterment of society. The United States was no longer going to influence the world alone. All the greatness of the Americas, Africa, Europe, and Asia was going to be consumed ad nauseum.

The result was far from the intention. In particular, the shift in economic thought pertaining to profit, scale, and competition has slowly transformed the social fabric that once held the United States in a cohesive bind. Technology accelerated this transformation by allowing transactions, services, and entire industries to detach from the communities they once served.

Take any transaction in the 21st century as your starting point. It involves one of the following aspects:

1. Global integration, whether that is the firm itself or the product
2. Subscription service or pay-later structure
3. Poor quality leading to increased need for replacement

The result? A feeling of being pushed to the brink. An illusion of financial choice. A cutthroat economic landscape where convenience often replaces durability and profit replaces obligation.

When the local baker served the entire town, they had the obligation to produce a quality product at a fair price. Why? Simple: the baker also relied on the town for their services in return. The banker wouldn’t squeeze an extra 10 basis points on the loan. The teacher had an obligation to educate the town to the best of their ability knowing those children would one day serve in those same roles.

The last thing anyone wanted was personal hostility with their neighbor. Their fellow churchgoers. Their children’s friends’ parents. The people that banded together to create the community.

Exporting business outside of the town meant the vessels that held the community together went with it. Transactions no longer required proximity, reputation, or long-term trust. This newly created cycle where businesses no longer feel obliged to create the best product but rather “just good enough.” You are charged the maximum amount possible wherever you go. Profits must be “maximized.” It allows PE firms to gut and liquidate mom-and-pop shops, or Blackrock to buy entire residential blocks. It is a faceless world making faceless transactions with whomever is on the other side of the screen.

The rise of globalization is a symptom of this shift: the gradual erosion of the communities that once structured everyday life. The world your parents grew up in was not perfect, but it was bound together by proximity, obligation, and shared identity. The modern world increasingly replaces those bonds with transactions.

And when a society replaces relationships with transactions, it slowly forgets what it was meant to be.
