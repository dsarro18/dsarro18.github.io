The Credentialed Median Voter

May 11, 2026

There is a popular meme chart on the internet focused on a bell curve with three individuals across the curve. The joke is that the high IQ and low IQ sides of the spectrum vote or act the same with brief, “based” responses, whereas the median individual is crying and complaining about something.

Is this oversimplified (obviously yes), and is this necessarily always true (again, obviously not). But it highlights discourse in America in the 21st century quite well.

The biggest shift in the last 20 years has been major voting bloc adjustments. Minority shifts toward Republicans, college-educated voters toward Democrats, etc. You’ve seen it everywhere if you follow election forecasts.

Obama’s presidency remains one of the most consequential of the past 50 years. Without diving into every facet, one proposal specifically symbolized an already accelerating trend: free community college.

While not adopted at the federal level, the proposal sparked reforms and discussion among public universities at the state level. Combine that with decades of increases in government-backed student loans, “No Child Left Behind” programs, degree pedigree deflation, declines in education quality, and simultaneous growth in administrative roles, and the result is a college system that is broader and more accessible than ever before, but arguably less selective and less intellectually rigorous.

If we extrapolate trends dating back to 1939, research from Mount Royal University suggests the average measured IQ of college graduates has declined meaningfully from earlier peaks of 119 to 101 in 2022.

Simply put, higher education 100 years ago was:

  1. More selective
  2. Higher quality
  3. More financially rewarding post-graduation

The contention that “IQ is not the only measure of intelligence” is obviously valid, but would one rationally argue that Zuckerberg, Gates, Kasparov, Damon, Kutcher, etc. lack intelligence despite unconventional educational paths? Of course not. They all also have high IQs, and there remains a link between career earnings and IQ. IQ remains an imperfect but still useful indicator of cognitive ability regardless of personal opinions or how said intelligence is applied.

Tie this back to the median college-educated voter, who also tends to be more civically engaged. They are voting Democrat more often than 20-30 years ago. Are Democrats suddenly getting “smarter voters” as they often imply?

Of course not, but the real contention point is that many of these voters believe they are smarter. This is where the danger lies. Educational credentials, job titles, and institutional prestige increasingly create the perception of superior political understanding whether or not it actually exists.

These individuals are bosses, professors (ironically where JD Vance’s famous comment originated regarding a University of Michigan professor using flawed annualized data comparisons and biased sample selection), teachers, community leaders, and parents.

The median voter’s political intelligence may not have changed much at all. They just have a $250k title attached to their name now.

And with money, credentials, and titles often comes the worst trait of all: smugness.

Good luck breaking through to them anytime soon.
