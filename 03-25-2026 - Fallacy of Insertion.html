Fallacy of Insertion

March 25, 2026

Most arguments for mass immigration rely on assumptions that are rarely stated, yet absolutely required.

First, that cultures are interchangeable. Second, that the receiving system has a real, unmet need.

Remove either, and the argument no longer stands. It has to be replaced with a different one entirely.

But that rarely happens.

Instead, the policy continues, detached from its own premises.

A useful way to see this is through substitution.

Imagine a system optimized over time - a team, a company, a nation. It develops identity through constraint: what it selects, what it rejects, what it refines.

Insertion is not neutral. It is not additive in a vacuum. It interacts with structure.

If what is inserted does not align, the system does not simply expand but rather it distorts.

If what is inserted is not needed, the system does not improve; it dilutes.

This is not a moral claim. It is a structural one.

Systems are coherent because they are selective. They persist because they filter.

When selection is replaced with indiscriminate intake, coherence degrades. Not immediately, but inevitably.

What makes this dynamic difficult to confront is that the justification often shifts.

When alignment is questioned, the argument becomes moral. When necessity is questioned, the argument becomes economic. When outcomes are questioned, the argument becomes emotional.

The premises move, but the policy remains.

Over time, reality accumulates.

Economic signals weaken. Social cohesion fragments. Political consensus disappears.

Individually, each can be rationalized. Together, they point to something systemic: a misalignment between input and structure.

The more interesting question is not whether the outcomes are good or bad.

It is why the system continues to operate as if its original assumptions still hold.

Most systems, when faced with contradiction, adapt accordingly.

They tighten constraints. They refine inputs. They correct course.

But when a system cannot question its own premises, it insteads persists to an undefined point.

And persistence, without alignment, is not stability.

It is drift into the unknown.
